It probably started after I finished [WISE](https://github.com/PKU-YuanGroup/WISE) last year: everyone who met me assumed I came from the generation camp. But in fact, from the very beginning of my research career, I have followed the classic multimodal understanding line of CLIP and LLaVA, and in my own mind I have always liked multimodal understanding a bit more. Over the past year, however, the center of gravity in the community seems to have leaned much more toward generation on the multimodal side (GPT-Image, Nanobanana, Sora, SeeDance...). When I talk with friends, many of them feel that multimodal understanding has basically already been solved, that the paradigm is very fixed, and that there is not much room left to keep going. And it is true that over the past two years, multimodal large models have advanced at extraordinary speed: image question answering, document understanding, video description, visual reasoning, GUI agents. More and more tasks have become capabilities that current models are simply expected to have. As an "extra ability" of LLMs, vision seems to have already met our requirements.
But if we look beyond today's benchmark performance and ask how far these systems still are from real-world intelligent systems, I still believe current multimodal understanding remains in a relatively early stage. Many models can already interpret an image, answer a video question, or carry out a multimodal dialogue. But that is still far from genuine world understanding. The real world is not a set of static pictures, not a few seconds of neatly clipped benchmark video, and certainly not a one-off multimodal input handed over by a user. The real world is continuous and flowing, physically unfolding, accumulated across time, and something that needs to be remembered, retrieved, judged, and acted upon.
Therefore, the future of multimodal understanding should not be understood merely as "models getting better at images" or "stronger at video." It should be understood as a kind of evolution: from static, offline, question-answering ability toward dynamic, online world-understanding systems with long-term memory, the ability to participate in decision-making, and the ability to collaborate with other agents. From this perspective, there are many directions, and many future possibilities, that excite me enormously.
1. Native Multimodality: The Real Starting Point of the Next Generation of Capabilities
Looking back at the last major wave of multimodal development, the LLaVA paradigm was undoubtedly a key milestone. It proved something extremely important: if you take a model that already has strong language ability, connect a vision encoder to it, and align the system with a relatively small amount of instruction data, you can obtain very respectable image understanding and multimodal dialogue ability. This paradigm is efficient in engineering terms, effective in practice, and it greatly accelerated the spread of multimodal capability.
But the LLaVA-style path is closer to an "interface-level fusion": the language model remains the core, and vision enters the system more as an attached module. It undeniably solved many problems, but it is hard for it to solve all problems. At its core, the recipe is still this: first use a vision encoder to extract visual features, then map those features into a token space the language model can understand. The model learns "how to treat visual information as language information," but it does not necessarily learn "how to form world knowledge in a visual way."
This is why the first main thread of future multimodal understanding must be a move toward native multimodality. And by "native" I mean at least two things.
The first is being native at the training level. That means bringing images, video, audio, and other modalities into unified modeling already at pretraining time, rather than first training a pure language model and only later patching in vision through adaptation and alignment. Only if multimodal signals participate jointly at the very beginning of knowledge formation can the model possibly build more natural cross-modal concepts at the level of underlying representations. In other words, vision is no longer just an "extra feature" of a language model; it becomes one of the model's sources of knowledge about the world.
The second is being native at the architectural level. A more aggressive future path may gradually weaken or even remove a standalone visual encoder. Instead of pre-packaging vision into high-level semantic features and then handing them to a language model, we may directly patchify pixels and treat visual tokens as native inputs to a unified model, letting the model itself learn within one architecture how to extract texture, boundaries, objects, relations, motion, and semantics from pixels. This idea is not merely a structural simplification; it is a shift in paradigm. The model is no longer "reading a pre-digested, language-like visual summary"; it is "understanding vision." This is also what our recent Neo series has been trying to explore (Neo https://github.com/EvolvingLMMs-Lab/NEO  series will have new updates very soon; stay tuned).
To be sure, the current paradigm can already solve many problems, but it is hard to believe it can solve all of them. My own bias is that only native multimodality can truly support scenarios like streaming video understanding, spatial intelligence, and physical consistency judgment in the real world. Native multimodality is not just "a more elegant implementation"; it is very likely the starting point for a whole series of crucial downstream capabilities.
Of course, this road is not easy. Unified modeling of vision directly at the patch level brings enormous computational burden. The information density and token structure across modalities differ far more than in pure language. And many questions remain open: whether training stability under a unified architecture can be maintained, whether scaling laws still hold, and so on. But the direction itself is already clear: if future multimodal understanding is to truly enter the world, it cannot remain forever at the stage of a "vision interface layer."
2. Streaming Video Understanding: Toward a World That Keeps Evolving
If native multimodality addresses how a model can truly possess multimodal representations, then streaming video understanding addresses another, more concrete question: how can a model face a world that keeps unfolding and never pauses? 
Today, many video understanding systems are still built on the frame-sampling paradigm. A video is treated as a sequence of sampled key frames, and the task is solved through image understanding, temporal aggregation, and language reasoning. This works well on many benchmarks and is enough to support video question answering, long-video summarization, action recognition, and many other tasks. But its limits are equally obvious: frame sampling preserves semantics, yet it often loses the most critical dynamic information. (BTW, I highly recommend LMMs-Lab's [OneVision-Encoder](https://github.com/EvolvingLMMs-Lab/OneVision-Encoder), which replaces traditional video frame sampling with codec-based encoding.)
A video is not a set of pictures; it is a slice of reality in the middle of happening. In the real world, a great deal of important information is not expressed by what a certain frame "looks like," but by how an object moves, how two objects make contact, how a state evolves, and how an action unfolds. Velocity, acceleration, collision, changes in occlusion, transmission of force, tiny deviations in motion, causal order: all of these are classic forms of dynamic information. Once the problem is reduced to sparse frame sampling, a model may still know that "there is a person, a table, and a cup in the scene," yet no longer truly know "how the cup was knocked over" or "whether the action was intentional or accidental."
This also means that the future of video understanding is not simply about sampling more frames from longer videos. It is about giving models streaming, online, continuous understanding. That matters especially in real-world settings. Vision Pro, AR glasses, robots, autonomous driving, industrial monitoring, medical monitoring: these are not problems of processing offline video files; they are problems of continuously receiving sensor streams. The input is not a few minutes of video, but an almost never-ending visual stream of the world.
In such settings, the model's core problems change accordingly. It is no longer just "watch first, answer later" or "answer when asked." It must continuously update its internal state as input arrives. It is not just summarizing content offline, but judging online what is happening now, what may happen next, which information should be kept, which changes deserve alerts, and which events form higher-level semantic units. This is closer to "real-time world modeling" than to video understanding in the conventional sense. From another angle, perhaps this is the OpenClaw of video understanding.
The difficulty of streaming video understanding begins with the fact that it requires models to handle short-term physical continuity and long-term semantic state at the same time. A model has to understand motion over hundreds of milliseconds while also maintaining situational state across minutes, hours, or even longer. Second, it requires incremental reasoning and memory compression. In the real world, it is impossible to recompute the entire history every time a new frame arrives, so the model must have mechanisms for event boundary detection, state summarization, local recomputation, writing to long-term memory, and so on. Finally, it must satisfy real-time constraints. That means understanding ability cannot be obtained indefinitely by simply piling on more compute; the system design itself has to rethink the balance among latency, memory, compression, and decision-making.
In the future, video understanding models will no longer be facing "a video clip," but "a world that keeps unfolding." And once that shift truly happens, multimodal understanding will, for the first time, genuinely move from media-content analysis to perception of real environments.
3. Long-Horizon Associative Memory: Event-Centric Memory for Multimodal Models
As noted above, if a model continuously receives a video stream, the next step cannot be to start understanding again every second; it must remember what has happened before. Pushing this one step further, the question becomes: can a model form human-like long-term visual memory over sufficiently long time scales?
This is a deeper problem than long-video understanding. Long-video understanding usually deals with continuous input spanning tens of minutes to a few hours, but many important real-world problems span days, weeks, or even longer. Multimodal models that truly move into personal devices, spatial computing, and embodied systems cannot live only inside single sessions and short windows. They must have the ability to keep accumulating experience, compress what they have gone through, build indices, and retrieve associated memories on demand.
The key is not to stuff all historical frames into an even longer context window. That is neither realistic nor intelligent. Human long-term memory is not a frame-by-frame replay of raw pixels either; it is highly event-centric, semantic, and relational. What we remember is not what the retina received every second over the last three days, but things like "I left the keys on the entryway cabinet yesterday," "the day before yesterday, I discussed a certain proposal with colleagues in front of the whiteboard in the meeting room," or "last week, the temperature curve looked abnormal before that experiment first went wrong." In other words, the core of long-term multimodal memory is not a bigger cache, but better event-centric memory, semantic indexing, and associative recall.
Once such a capability lands, the applications become extremely rich. The most typical example is future AR devices and personal intelligent assistants. Imagine a user wearing a device like Vision Pro and living and working with it continuously for several days. Then the user can naturally ask: "When was the last time I saw that red-covered copy of Anna Karenina in the office the day before yesterday?" "When we discussed the whiteboard plan with my advisor last week, what were the final two revisions he asked us to make?" "Over the past few days, after I finish my coffee each day, roughly how long does it take before I start losing focus?" "Did I put that package in the study or in the living room?" None of these are questions that traditional multimodal question answering can solve. They require the model to span days of visual stream, track people, objects, locations, and events, build indices automatically, and make cross-scene semantic associations.
Another example is the home-robot scenario. A truly useful companion or assistive robot must know whether an elderly person has taken their medicine on time over the past few days, whether their recent movement patterns are unusual, where a certain object is usually placed, and whether some behavioral pattern has changed. This is no longer simple surveillance-video playback; it is a long-term multimodal memory system with semantic extraction, pattern association, and causal backtracking.
In a certain sense, future multimodal understanding will gradually become something like growing up alongside humans. It does not just perceive the world; it must accompany the world through time. It must know what is worth remembering, what is worth forgetting, what should be written into long-term memory as common sense, what should remain only as short-term state, and what should be reactivated when some future question appears. A long-term memory mechanism may become the bridge that takes multimodal systems from demos to real assistants.
4. Multimodal Retrieval: The Next Generation of Search Engines?
Once a model begins to perceive continuously and form memories, the next core question becomes: how can those memories be called up effectively? If a model can understand but cannot index, store, retrieve, and recombine the results of that understanding effectively, then its value will remain confined to "answering on the spot" rather than becoming reusable information over time.
This is exactly why multimodal retrieval, especially video retrieval, will, in my view, become extremely important in the future.
For the past few decades of the internet, text has been the main carrier of knowledge, so retrieval systems have primarily served web pages, documents, and text content. Search engines match and rank text; recommender systems model user preferences over text and structured content. But as bandwidth improves, devices become ubiquitous, and generative technology advances, video is increasingly becoming the dominant form of information transmission. Tutorials, meetings, livestreams, classes, first-person recordings, workflow demos, social expression, and real-world capture will increasingly accumulate as video. Much of the most important knowledge on the future internet may not be written on web pages at all; it may be hidden inside countless hours of video streams. (This point was inspired by a podcast from Elon Musk.)
At that point, the value of multimodal understanding is no longer just helping users describe what a video says. It has to answer more concrete and more difficult questions: At exactly what minute in which video did a certain operation happen? Are there other videos showing a similar physical process? Is the scene I just saw similar to some clip you recorded last week? When a user says "something like yesterday," can the system find the corresponding case from days of visual records? In that Gojo-vs.-Sukuna video using "Rainie Love" as the BGM, at what second does Gojo use ...
Compared with image-text retrieval, video retrieval is inherently harder, because video has not only content but time; not only objects but actions; not only semantics but event unfolding; not only visuals but speech, subtitles, ambient sound, and contextual state. A truly strong video retrieval system does not just assign a single label to an entire clip. It builds multi-granularity indices at the shot level, segment level, event level, and video level, and it supports cross-modal queries: users can use text to find video, video to find video, or some past experience to find similar cases for a current problem.
From this perspective, multimodal retrieval will become the key bridge between understanding ability and knowledge systems. The most valuable multimodal systems in the future may not be the ones best at instant description, but the ones best at remembering and finding things again. Because many times, what we truly need is not an explanation, but a precise recall: bringing back something that happened in the past but has already left the current context.
5. Multimodal Understanding as Critic: Supervising World Models and Visual Generation
As people who work on unified multimodal understanding and generation, we always hope to find ways that understanding can benefit generation and generation can benefit understanding. And I have always held a view that may or may not be correct.
I actually think understanding has been benefiting generation for quite a while already. Much of the current progress in multimodal generation comes from the strength of multimodal understanding: in the mid-training stage, multimodal understanding models label the data; in the post-training stage, multimodal understanding models serve as reward models.
Visual generation is now advancing rapidly and continuously. More and more models can generate high-fidelity dynamic visual content from text, images, or action conditions. But at least for now, the weak spots of visual generation are often exactly the weak spots of visual understanding. For example, visual generation can already deliver strong realism in visual style and short-term local consistency, but once the requirement is raised to the level of physics and temporal coherence, the problems are quickly exposed. Objects suddenly interpenetrate, contact relations become unstable, the logic before and after an action breaks, character identity drifts, occlusion relations go wrong, and causal order becomes confused. These are precisely the places where multimodal understanding is still insufficient. As one of the data sources for generative models (which is admittedly a slightly funny thing to say out loud) and as the critic in post-training, the power of multimodal generation will inevitably depend on a stronger multimodal understanding model.
There is one example that left a particularly strong impression on me. When Sora 2 came out last October, I ran an evaluation across several strong models available at the time, including SeeDance and Veo3. The task was very simple: depict a little boy entering a shop to buy flowers, then leaving the shop and closing the door. But every model got stuck on that final motion where the boy turns around, exits, and closes the door. What shocked me even more was that when I used visual understanding models to judge the outputs, every one of them thought there was nothing wrong with the video.
Similar problems also appear in image scoring. Recently I have been rebuilding WISE, and in about two weeks I will probably release a new version of WISE (not some WISE 2.0, just a verified version). I needed to use open-source VLMs to score images generated by models. But after going through everything from Qwen2.5 to all Qwen3.5 models below 200B, I found that only Qwen3.5-122B-A10B reached a level that was even somewhat usable...
This shows that current multimodal understanding still falls short of what we need when it serves as a visual critic.
I think such a critic needs to operate on at least three levels. The first is the semantic level: does the generated content match the text instruction, scene setup, and object relations? The second is the temporal level: do events unfold naturally, are actions continuous, are state transitions reasonable, and are objects and events maintained in "memory"? The third is the physical level: do gravity, inertia, collision, support, contact, occlusion, and physical affordances obey basic laws of the world? And finally, based on semantics, temporality, and physics, can the model predict the likelihood of what happens next?
Going one step further, a sufficiently strong critic should not be just an examiner that outputs a score. It should be an interpretable diagnostician. It should not only say "this video is bad," but also explain what is bad about it: at which second a contact relation breaks down, which object loses identity consistency across frames, which segment of motion violates kinematic common sense, or which scene transition breaks an implicit causal chain. In this way, multimodal understanding models can further guide the training and optimization direction of video generation.
It can be said that if world models are responsible for imagining the world, then multimodal understanding models will be responsible for evaluating the world. And a video-understanding critic that truly understands physics, temporality, and interaction may become a necessary precondition for the next leap in world-model quality.
6. Spatial Intelligence and Embodied Decision-Making: From Chat Models to Action Models
Judging from the performance of today's multimodal models, an increasingly obvious tension is emerging: models are improving quickly at semantic understanding and conversation, but remain relatively weak at pixel-level detail, geometric structure, spatial constraints, and action decision-making.
This limitation mainly comes from three levels. First, the architectural level. Most models rely on CLIP or similar vision encoders for visual representations, and such modules are themselves oriented more toward high-level semantic compression and cross-modal alignment than toward preserving fine-grained pixel information, 3D geometric structure, or spatial manipulability. Second, the data level. Most current training data consists of images or videos paired with text descriptions. The supervisory signal emphasizes how a scene can be described, rather than how a scene is organized in space, how objects constrain one another, and how actions are realized in the physical world. Finally, the objective level. The optimization target of most MLLMs is still, in essence, to fit a language distribution under multimodal conditions. So what models ultimately learn is a capability for "speaking better when they see an image," not a capability for "acting better when they see the world."
Of course, I am not an expert in this area, so I will not speculate too much about what the future should look like here.
7. The Real Challenge: Problem Definitions and Evaluation Closer to the Real World
If we place all of these trends side by side, we find that the key to the future of multimodal understanding is not continued improvement on current benchmarks; the research objective itself is being rewritten.
In the past, multimodal evaluation mainly revolved around image question answering, short-video understanding, OCR, chart reasoning, and multimodal knowledge question answering. These tasks are of course important, and they have provided necessary traction for capability development. But they still belong largely to a paradigm of "static samples, offline answers." The tasks that will really matter in the future may instead be hours-long or even days-long streaming understanding, cross-scene long-term memory retrieval, physical consistency judgment, 3D spatial reasoning, embodied action decision-making, collaborative perception, and state maintenance.
This means that one of the greatest bottlenecks facing future multimodal understanding may not be the model itself, but the fact that we still have not established evaluation systems sufficiently aligned with real-world tasks. A model may be very strong on benchmarks, yet unable to continuously track an object for three days in the real world. It may answer physics multiple-choice questions correctly, yet fail to identify where a generated video violates contact rules. It may describe the layout of a room, yet still fail to support a robot in safely completing a grasp. Evaluation shapes optimization direction, and if the target always remains "more like an exam question," then models will struggle to become "more like the world."
At the same time, another frequently overlooked issue is memory management. Real-world input is an infinite stream, while any system's compute, bandwidth, and context are finite. So one of the core capabilities of future multimodal understanding must be to know what to remember, what to forget, what to compress, what to index, and what to recall again, and when. And how to evaluate such a capability and design a relatively reasonable and general scenario for it is itself a test for us.
After all, defining the problem has always been one of the most important abilities in research.
In the End
Beyond all this, there are many, many more possibilities, such as multi-agent perceptual collaboration and interactive understanding. In short, if countless people are already convinced that the future of large language models is a transformation of productivity in the digital world, then I believe the future of the multimodal world will be the intelligent gateway to the real world.
